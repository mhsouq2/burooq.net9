How Many CCTV Cameras Do I Need for My Villa, Apartment or Office?

A complete, no-fluff breakdown of how many security cameras you actually need, by property type, with placement diagrams, budget impact and the mistakes that leave blind spots. Written by our installation engineers after hundreds of site surveys across Abu Dhabi.

If you searched "how many CCTV cameras do I need", you've probably already gotten three different answers from three different camera sellers, each conveniently matching whatever package they happen to sell. The honest answer is that it depends on your property's layout, but after hundreds of site surveys across Abu Dhabi villas, apartments and offices, we can give you a real range to work from, and explain exactly what pushes that number up or down.

This guide covers camera counts by property type, the placement logic that matters more than the number itself, how resolution trades off against camera count, what it costs at each tier, and the most common mistakes that leave properties with expensive systems and real blind spots.

Quick answer: typical camera counts by property type

Small villa (2–3 bed)

4–6 cameras: main gate, front door, rear access, garden.

Large villa (4–5 bed)

6–10 cameras: multiple entrances, driveway, pool/majlis, perimeter corners.

Apartment

1–3 cameras: front door, balcony/terrace if accessible from outside.

Small office / shop

4–8 cameras: entrance, till/reception, stock room, parking.

Warehouse

8–20+ cameras: loading bays, racking aisles, perimeter fencing, office area.

Multi-tenant building

Varies by floor: lobby, lift lobbies, fire exits, parking, roof access.

These are starting ranges, not final numbers. The next sections explain exactly why a number changes for your specific property, and our free site survey confirms the exact figure before you pay for anything.

How many cameras does a villa actually need? A room-by-room logic

Instead of thinking in total camera count, think in coverage points: each camera should answer one specific question, "who is at this door," "is anyone approaching this gate," "can I see the full driveway." Most villas need coverage at:

  • Main vehicle gate — wide-angle, capturing plates and faces as vehicles approach.
  • Front pedestrian door — close-range, face-height angle for clear identification.
  • Rear or side access — often the most overlooked entry point and the one intruders prefer.
  • Garden / majlis / pool area — a separate wide camera if this area is used for entertaining or is visible from outside the perimeter wall.
  • Driveway / parking — especially important if vehicles or bikes are stored outside.
  • Roof access points — for villas with external stairs or low walls bordering neighboring plots.

Count your villa's actual access points and outdoor areas using this list, and you'll land very close to the real number before any salesperson gives you one.

What pushes your camera count up

Three things consistently increase the number beyond the basic range above:

  1. Multiple vehicle entrances

    Corner villas or properties with a service gate need separate coverage at each entrance, not one camera trying to cover both.

  2. Irregular building shape

    L-shaped villas, extensions and boundary walls at angles create corners a single camera physically cannot see around.

  3. Areas you want monitored separately from security

    A pool you want to check on for child safety, or a majlis where you want footage of guests arriving, are separate use-cases from perimeter security and often justify their own camera.

Resolution vs. camera count: the trade-off nobody explains

A common and expensive mistake is assuming more cameras always means better security. In reality, placement and resolution matter more than raw count. A single well-placed 4K camera covering a long driveway can identify a face or plate at a distance that two cheaper 2MP cameras would render as an unrecognizable blur.

As a rule of thumb: use higher resolution (4K/8MP) on your one or two highest-priority points (main gate, front door), and standard HD (2–4MP) on secondary coverage areas like garden corners and side access, where general activity detection matters more than fine detail.

Don't forget the recorder: storage capacity matters as much as camera count

Every additional camera, and every increase in resolution, adds to how much storage your recorder (NVR) needs to retain footage for your chosen number of days. A system with the "right" camera count but an undersized hard drive will silently overwrite your oldest footage long before you expect it to. What this means for your budget

Camera count is usually the single biggest line item in a CCTV quote, so getting it right, rather than over-buying "just in case" or under-buying to save money upfront, has the biggest impact on value. Over-installing creates redundant coverage and wasted storage cost; under-installing leaves blind spots that defeat the purpose of the system entirely. This is exactly why we run a free site survey before quoting: it's the only reliable way to land on the number your specific property needs, not a generic package size.

Common mistakes we see in villa CCTV systems

  • One camera trying to cover two entrances at a wide angle, resulting in low detail on both.
  • Cameras pointed into direct sunrise/sunset glare, washing out footage at the exact times lighting matters most.
  • No camera on the side or service access, the point most often used for unauthorized entry.
  • Buying camera count based on price, not coverage, then discovering blind spots after something happens.
  • Undersized storage, meaning footage from three weeks ago no longer exists when you need it.

Getting the exact number for your property

Every number in this guide is a planning range. The only way to get an exact, guaranteed-accurate count is a walk-through of your actual property, where an engineer checks access points, lighting conditions for night recording, and cable routing before confirming a camera count and fixed-price quotation.
